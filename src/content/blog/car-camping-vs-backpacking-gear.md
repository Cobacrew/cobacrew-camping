title: "Car camping vs backpacking: what gear you actually need"
description: "Why backpacking gear guides don't apply to car camping, and where you can save real money by ignoring ultralight advice."
pubDate: 2026-07-09
---

A lot of camping gear advice online is written by and for backpackers, where every ounce matters because you're carrying it for miles. If you're car camping, most of that advice actively costs you money for no benefit. Here's where the two diverge.

## Where weight stops mattering

Backpackers pay a premium for lightweight materials — a 2 lb tent instead of a 10 lb tent can cost 3–4x more. If your gear goes from the trunk to the campsite and back, that weight difference is irrelevant. You're paying for an advantage you can't use.

This applies to:

- **Tents** — buy for weather resistance and space, not pack weight
- **Sleeping pads** — a thicker, heavier foam or self-inflating pad is more comfortable than an ultralight one, and costs less
- **Cookware** — bring an actual pot and pan, not a nesting titanium mess kit
- **Chairs** — camp chairs don't exist in the backpacking world at all for a reason; bring real ones

## Where backpacking advice still applies

Some fundamentals hold regardless of how you're getting to the campsite:

- **Layer for temperature swings** rather than buying one do-everything jacket
- **A sleeping pad matters for warmth**, not just comfort, in both contexts
- **Buy a tent rated for one more person than your group** to have room for gear

## What car camping lets you add that backpackers can't

This is where car camping actually gets to be more comfortable, not just "backpacking but lazier":

- **A real cooler** instead of freeze-dried meals
- **Camp chairs and a table**
- **A propane double-burner stove** instead of a single backpacking burner
- **Extra water** — you're not limited by what you can carry, so bring more than you think you need
- **A rug or mat for the tent entrance**, extra blankets, a real pillow

## The mistake that costs beginners the most money

The most expensive mistake is buying backpacking-grade gear for a car camping trip because ultralight brands dominate gear review sites and social media. A $400 ultralight tent does the exact same job at a campsite as a $120 car camping tent — the only difference is 8 lbs you're never going to carry anywhere. Skip the backpacking aisle entirely unless you know you're going to start backpacking specifically.

## When it's worth crossing over

If you're testing whether you'll like backpacking eventually, it's reasonable to buy one or two dual-purpose items — a good sleeping bag or pad that would work in either context — rather than buying twice. But don't buy backpacking gear preemptively for trips you're not actually taking yet.
