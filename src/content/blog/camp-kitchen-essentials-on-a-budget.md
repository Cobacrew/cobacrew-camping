You don't need a dedicated "camp kitchen" product line to eat well outdoors. Most of what you need either doubles from your regular kitchen or costs very little new. Here's the actual list, split by what's worth buying specifically for camping and what you can just bring from home.

## Cooking

- **Two-burner propane stove.** This is the one item genuinely worth buying camping-specific. It's more stable and controllable than a single backpacking burner, and lets you cook two things at once — which matters more than people expect once you're feeding more than one person.
- **One pot, one pan.** Buy these camping-specific too — they're built for direct flame and open-air cooking, and they're light enough that it's not worth dragging heavy kitchen cookware.
- **Extra propane canisters.** Bring one more than you think you'll need. Running out mid-trip with no backup is one of the most common camp kitchen failures.

## Prep and storage

- **A cutting board** — any board from home works fine.
- **A knife.** One decent knife, not a full set.
- **Reusable food storage containers or bags** for pre-prepped ingredients. Prepping vegetables and marinades at home before you leave saves a huge amount of camp time and mess.
- **A cooler with a drain plug** for anything that needs to stay cold — see our full [cooler guide](/blog/best-camping-coolers-under-100/) for what to look for.

## Cleanup

- **A collapsible dish bin or basin.** This is the item beginners forget most often, and then end up scrubbing pans in a tiny sink or a stream.
- **Biodegradable camp soap.** Regular dish soap can harm water sources if it washes into them — biodegradable soap costs about the same and means you're not restricted on where you can dump wash water (still dump it away from water sources regardless).
- **A small sponge or scrub brush**, and a dish towel.
- **Trash bags** — more than you think. Double-bag food waste to keep animals away from your site.

## What to skip

- **Specialty camp coffee makers.** A basic pour-over cone or a French press you already own works fine. You don't need a $40 percolator for your first few trips.
- **Camp-specific plates and utensils**, unless you want them. Plastic or metal plates from home work fine — just make sure they're not something you mind getting scratched up.
- **A full spice kit.** Bring 4–5 spices you actually cook with in small containers, not a 12-piece rack.

## A simple approach for your first trip

Plan meals that use one pot and minimal prep — chili, foil-packet meals, pasta with a jarred sauce, breakfast burritos. Save the ambitious multi-course camp cooking for once you've got a few trips under your belt and know what your setup can actually handle.
