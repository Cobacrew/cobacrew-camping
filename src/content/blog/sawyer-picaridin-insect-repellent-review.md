## What Picaridin actually is

Picaridin is a synthetic compound modeled after a compound found in pepper plants. It's been registered with the EPA since the early 2000s and is the standard repellent recommended throughout Europe and Australia, where it's more common than DEET. The CDC lists it as an effective repellent against mosquitoes and ticks, on par with DEET at comparable concentrations.

The practical difference most people notice isn't effectiveness — it's everything else:

- **No greasy or sticky feel.** DEET sprays often leave a film; Picaridin dries down clean.
- **Doesn't damage plastics, synthetics, or gear coatings.** DEET is notorious for degrading rain jacket coatings, watch faces, and some plastics. Picaridin doesn't do this, which matters if you're spraying it near a tent, pack, or clothing rather than just skin.
- **Low odor.** DEET has a distinct chemical smell; Picaridin is close to scentless.

## What it doesn't do differently

Picaridin isn't "stronger" than DEET — at equivalent concentrations, independent testing (including CDC-cited studies) shows them performing similarly against mosquitoes and ticks over comparable time windows. If someone tells you Picaridin repels bugs DEET can't, that's not accurate. The reason to choose it is comfort and gear-safety, not a effectiveness upgrade.
