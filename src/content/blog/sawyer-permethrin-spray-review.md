## This is not a skin repellent — and that's the point

Permethrin is not sprayed on skin. It's applied to clothing, boots, tents, and gear, where it chemically bonds to fabric fibers. Ticks and mosquitoes that contact treated fabric are killed or repelled on contact, rather than just smelling something unpleasant and moving on. This is a meaningfully different mechanism than DEET or Picaridin, which work by repelling insects away from skin.

The practical upside: once applied, a single treatment lasts through **about 6 weeks or 6 washes** on clothing — you're not reapplying every time you head out, the way you would with skin spray.

## Why this matters more than most people realize

Skin repellent handles exposed skin. It does nothing for a tick that lands on your pant leg, walks up through a sock, or rides in on a jacket sleeve — which is how most tick attachments actually happen. The CDC specifically recommends permethrin-treated clothing as one of the most effective tick-prevention measures available to the public, more effective against ticks specifically than skin repellent alone. If you camp or hike in tick-prone regions, this is arguably a higher-value purchase than the skin spray sitting next to it on the shelf.

## How to actually use it

- Spray in a well-ventilated area, ideally outdoors, and let treated items dry fully (2–4 hours) before wearing.
- Focus on pant cuffs, socks, sleeves, and collar — the entry points ticks actually use.
- It's safe for most synthetic and natural fabrics, but check care labels for anything delicate.
- It's inactive on dry skin once fully dried, but it should never be applied directly to skin — it's a fabric-only product.

## What buyers report

The most common theme in reviews is people wishing they'd started using it sooner, especially after a tick scare. The 6-week/6-wash duration gets consistent praise for being genuinely low-maintenance compared to reapplying skin spray daily. The main adjustment new buyers need to make is patience — treated gear needs real drying time before use, and skipping that step is the most common misuse.

## Bottom line

Pair this with a skin repellent rather than choosing one or the other — they cover different exposure points and neither fully substitutes for the other. For tick-prone trails specifically, Permethrin-treated clothing is one of the highest-value, lowest-effort prevention steps a camper can take.
