Comfort gear doesn't affect whether your trip is safe or functional the way shelter and sleep gear does — but it has an outsized effect on whether people actually enjoy themselves. Here's what's worth spending on under $50, and what to skip.

## Camp chairs: worth it immediately

Sitting on a cooler or a log for a full weekend gets old by the second afternoon. A decent camp chair costs $20–35 and dramatically changes how comfortable downtime at the site feels. Look for:

- **A weight rating that comfortably exceeds the heaviest person using it** — cheap chairs sometimes list a rating that's technically true but not comfortable in practice
- **A cup holder and side pocket**, which sounds minor but gets used constantly
- **A carry bag**, so it's not loose gear rattling around the trunk

Buy one per person rather than a couple of "extra" chairs — mismatched seating (some people standing, some sitting) is a small thing that adds up over a weekend.

## A folding table: worth it if you're cooking real meals

If your camp kitchen involves more than heating one pot, a $25–40 folding table makes food prep dramatically easier than working off a cooler lid or the ground. Skip this if you're doing very simple one-pot meals for a short trip — the picnic table most developed campsites provide is enough.

## A rug or mat for the tent entrance

A cheap outdoor mat ($10–15) at your tent's entrance keeps a surprising amount of dirt and moisture out of your sleeping area. This is a small purchase that has an outsized effect on how clean and dry your tent stays, especially after rain.

## What's nice but skippable on a first trip

- **Hammocks.** Genuinely nice, but not essential, and setup depends heavily on having the right trees.
- **Camp rugs for the whole site.** A nice touch once you're camping regularly; not a first-trip priority.
- **Specialty camp pillows.** A pillow from home in a pillowcase you don't mind getting dirty works fine.
- **Portable speakers.** Some campgrounds restrict noise anyway — check the rules before assuming you'll want one.

## Bottom line

Prioritize a chair per person first — it's the single comfort item with the best return per dollar. Add a table if your cooking setup needs one. Everything else on this list is a "once you know you'll keep camping" purchase, not a first-trip requirement.
