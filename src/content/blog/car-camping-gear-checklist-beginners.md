If you're car camping instead of backpacking, weight doesn't matter — but that also means it's easy to overspend on gear you don't need. This list covers what actually matters for a first trip, split into "get this" and "skip this for now."

## Shelter

- **A tent rated for one more person than your group.** A "4-person" tent fits four sleeping bags with zero room for gear. Size up.
- **A footprint or cheap tarp** cut to size and placed under the tent. Protects the floor from rocks and moisture — a $15 tarp is cheaper than a torn tent floor.
- **Extra stakes.** Tents ship with the bare minimum. A 10-pack of all-purpose stakes costs almost nothing and saves a trip when two bend on the first try.

Skip: a second "vestibule" tent or screen room for your first trip. Nice to have, not essential.

## Sleep system

- **Sleeping bag rated 10–15°F below the coldest night you expect.** Bag temperature ratings are optimistic; buy colder than you think you need.
- **Sleeping pad**, even in a tent with a floor. The ground pulls heat out of your body faster than cold air does — this is the piece beginners skip and regret.
- **A pillow**, or a stuff sack filled with a jacket. Don't buy a special camp pillow yet.

## Camp kitchen

- **A two-burner propane stove.** More reliable and easier to cook real meals on than a single backpacking burner.
- **One pot, one pan.** Buy a basic camp cookware set rather than dragging kitchen pots — they're lighter and made for direct flame.
- **A cooler with drain plug** and reusable ice packs. This is worth spending a bit more on — a mediocre cooler means food spoils on day two.
- **Basic utensils, a cutting board, and a dish bin.** A lot of first-timers forget dish soap and a way to wash up.

## Lighting

- **A headlamp per person.** Hands-free light matters more than people expect until they're trying to cook dinner in the dark holding a phone flashlight.
- **One lantern** for the picnic table or inside the tent.

Skip: string lights and multiple lanterns. One good light source per zone is enough.

## Comfort and extras

- **Camp chairs.** Sitting on a cooler for a weekend gets old fast.
- **A basic first aid kit**, plus any personal medication.
- **Trash bags** — bring more than you think, and pack out everything.

## What to actually spend money on

If you're on a budget, put your money into the cooler and the sleeping pad first. Those two are the difference between a trip that feels miserable and one that feels comfortable, and they're the hardest to substitute with something you already own. Everything else can start cheap and get upgraded once you know you'll keep camping.
