Premium rotomolded coolers can hold ice for a week and cost $300+. For a weekend or two a month, that's overkill. Here's what to look for under $100, and where the real tradeoffs are.

## What actually determines ice retention

Ice retention comes down to three things, roughly in order of importance:

1. **Wall thickness and insulation type.** Thicker foam walls hold cold longer. Budget coolers use thinner walls than premium ones — that's the single biggest reason a $300 cooler beats a $60 one on multi-day trips.
2. **Lid seal.** A gasket that actually seals flush matters more than most buyers expect. Check reviews for complaints about lids not sealing tight.
3. **How often it's opened.** This matters more than the cooler itself for a lot of trips. Every time the lid opens, cold air spills out. A cheaper cooler used carefully will outperform an expensive one opened forty times a day.

## What's realistic at this price

At $60–100, expect solid 2–3 day ice retention with normal use (occasional lid opening, not left in direct sun). That covers the overwhelming majority of car camping trips. If you're doing 5+ day trips regularly, that's when the jump to a premium rotomolded cooler starts to pay for itself — but that's a "later" purchase, not a "starting out" one.

## Non-negotiables at this price

- **A drain plug.** Trying to dump melted ice water out of a cooler without one is miserable. Skip any budget cooler that doesn't have one.
- **Handles that won't snap.** Check reviews specifically for handle complaints — this is where budget coolers cut corners.
- **A latch that holds the lid shut**, especially if you're driving on rough roads with the cooler in the trunk.

## How to actually get 3 days out of a $70 cooler

- **Pre-chill the cooler** before loading it — run cold water in it or leave ice in it for an hour before you load food.
- **Use block ice, not cubed**, for the base layer. Block ice melts slower because it has less surface area. Add cubed ice on top for quick access to cold drinks.
- **Pack it full.** A full cooler stays cold longer than a half-full one — the empty air space is what warms up fastest. Use a extra bag of ice or a rolled towel to fill gaps if you're not packing much food.
- **Keep it out of direct sun** and off hot pavement. A cooler sitting in the shade on grass will outlast the same cooler sitting on asphalt in the sun by a full day or more.

## Bottom line

For most car camping trips, a well-reviewed $60–100 cooler with a real drain plug and decent seal, used with the packing tricks above, will do the job a $300 cooler does — just for one or two fewer days. Save the upgrade for when you're actually taking longer trips.
