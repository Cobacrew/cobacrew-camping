Gear gets most of the attention, but picking the wrong campsite causes more first-trip disappointment than any piece of gear ever does. Here's what actually matters.

## Start closer than feels necessary

For a first trip, pick a campground within an hour or two of home, not the most scenic spot you can find three states away. Being close means:

- If something goes wrong (forgotten gear, bad weather, a kid who hates it), you can leave without losing a whole trip
- You get a low-stakes trial run for your gear and routine before committing to somewhere remote
- You spend less of the weekend driving and more of it actually camping

Save the ambitious, far-flung destination for once you know your setup works.

## Developed vs. primitive sites

For a first trip, choose a **developed campground** — one with designated sites, potable water, and (ideally) bathrooms — over primitive or dispersed camping. Developed sites remove several variables at once: you don't need to worry about water treatment, site selection for drainage and flatness, or waste management beyond what the campground provides. Primitive camping is a great next step once you're comfortable, not a great starting point.

## What to check before booking

- **Site reviews mentioning noise, shade, and ground condition** — a site description alone often won't tell you it's gravel, sloped, or right next to the generator area
- **Distance from the bathroom/water source**, if that matters to your group — too close means noise and traffic, too far means a long walk at night
- **Whether reservations are required.** Popular campgrounds fill up weeks or months ahead for summer weekends — check well before you plan to go
- **Fire and generator rules**, especially during dry seasons when burn bans are common

## Weather matters more than the destination

Check the forecast for your actual dates before finalizing anything, not just the general climate for the season. A gorgeous campground in a surprise rainstorm with no shelter plan is a rough first experience. If rain is likely, prioritize sites with some tree cover or a covered picnic area over ones without.

## The mistake that ruins the most first trips

Booking a site that's too ambitious — too remote, too primitive, or too far — before knowing whether you and your group actually enjoy camping yet. Pick something easy and forgiving for trip one. If it goes well, the ambitious trips are still there waiting for trip three or four.
