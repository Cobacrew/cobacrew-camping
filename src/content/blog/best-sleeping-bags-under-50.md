Sleeping bags are one of the few items where getting it wrong doesn't just mean discomfort — a bag that's too warm-rated for the night you're actually camping means a genuinely miserable, cold sleep. Here's how to buy right under $50.

## Ignore the exact number, respect the direction

Temperature ratings on budget bags are often optimistic — a "30°F" bag might realistically keep an average sleeper comfortable down to 40°F, not 30°F. The fix isn't to distrust ratings entirely, it's to **buy 10–15°F colder than the lowest temperature you expect**. If you're camping in nights that dip to 45°F, look for a bag rated around 30°F, not 45°F.

## What matters at this price

- **Shape.** Rectangular bags are roomier and cheaper; mummy-shaped bags trap heat better because there's less empty air space to warm up. For car camping in mild conditions, rectangular is fine and more comfortable. For anything approaching freezing, a mummy shape performs noticeably better for the same rated temperature.
- **Fill type.** Budget bags almost always use synthetic fill, not down. That's fine — synthetic fill is cheaper, still insulates when damp (down loses almost all insulating power when wet), and is the right choice at this price point regardless.
- **A draft collar or hood**, if you're buying for genuinely cold nights. Bags without one lose heat fast around the neck and shoulders. Not essential for mild summer car camping.

## The pad matters as much as the bag

This is the single most common mistake: buying a great sleeping bag and skipping the sleeping pad. Ground contact pulls heat out of your body far faster than air does, no matter how good the bag is. Even in warm weather, a pad makes a noticeable comfort difference — in cool weather, it's not optional. A basic foam or budget inflatable pad under $30 dramatically improves what a $40 bag can actually deliver.

## Layering instead of overbuying

Rather than buying one expensive bag rated for every possible temperature, it's cheaper and more flexible to buy a mid-range bag and layer:

- Wear a base layer to bed on cold nights instead of buying a colder-rated bag
- Bring a **liner** ($15–20) that adds roughly 5–15°F of warmth to any bag — this is often better value than upgrading to a warmer bag entirely
- Use a blanket over the bag on unexpectedly cold nights rather than buying for the coldest possible scenario

## Bottom line

At this price, buy a synthetic-fill bag rated 10–15°F colder than you expect to need, pair it with even a basic sleeping pad, and consider a liner instead of a warmer (and pricier) bag if you camp in variable conditions.
